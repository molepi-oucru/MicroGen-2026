  1. NCBI Resources: NCBI
  2. Artemis Genome Browser: Artemis

Let’s begin with the first and most powerful resource in bacterial genomics, the NCBI.

1. NCBI & BLAST

The National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) is a public bioinformatics platform that provides access to biological data and tools for analyzing DNA, RNA, and protein sequences. It hosts major databases such as GenBank (nucleotide sequences), RefSeq (curated reference genomes and genes), SRA (raw sequencing data), and protein resources, making it a central resource for genomic research and education.

One of the most widely used NCBI tools is BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool), which compares a query sequence against database sequences to find regions of similarity. BLAST helps identify genes, predict function, detect homologs, and assess evolutionary relationships. It works by producing local alignments and ranking results based on similarity scores and statistical significance (E-values).Different BLAST programs are used depending on the analysis type: BLASTn (DNA vs DNA), BLASTp (protein vs protein), BLASTx (translated DNA vs protein), and tBLASTn (protein vs translated DNA). These options allow flexible analysis of genomic and proteomic data.

In genomics, BLAST is commonly used for sequence identification, annotation, and comparative analysis. By finding similar sequences across organisms, it supports studies of gene function, evolutionary relationships, and phylogeny. It is often the first step in genomic analysis before more detailed alignment and tree-building methods.

1.2 NCBI Practice 2

Objectives

  • Use NCBI to search and download protein sequences and annotations
  • Compare the protein sequences and download the Results Table

Background

You will study the similarities of the HA (Hemagglutinin) protein from seasonal and avian influenza viruses. Influenza viruses are classified based on the antigenic characteristics of their surface glycoproteins—hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA). In Influenza A virus, 18 HA subtypes and 11 NA subtypes have been identified, and the HA subtypes are further grouped into two major evolutionary lineages: Group 1 (including H1, H2, H5, and others) and Group 2 (including H3, H4, H7, and others), based on phylogenetic analysis of the HA gene.

Now, let’s download the HA protein sequences of H1N1, H3N2, H5N1 and H7N9 from NCBI.

Tasks

  1. Go to NCBI
  2. Select “Protein Database”
  3. Search with key words “influenza A H1N1 hemagglutinin”
  4. Filter to “RefSeq” and select a complete HA protein sequence from a representitive H1N1 strain
  5. Record the accession number (i.e YP_009118626.1)
  6. Download the protein sequence: “Send to” –> “File” –> “Format” –> “FASTA” –> “Create File”

Congratulations! 🎉 You have successfully downloaded the HA protein sequence of H1N1.

  1. Repeat step 3-6 with key words “influenza A H3N2 hemagglutinin”
  2. Repeat step 3-6 with key words “influenza A H5N1 hemagglutinin”
  3. Repeat step 3-6 with key words “influenza A H7N9 hemagglutinin”

Congratulations! 🎉 You have now downloaded the HA protein sequences of H1N1, H3N2, H5N1 and H7N9.

Now, let’s compare the protein sequences of H1N1, H3N2, H5N1 and H7N9 using BLAST.

  1. Go to NCBI and Select “Protein BLAST”
  2. Select “Align two or more sequences”
  3. Compare the protein sequence of H1 (query) with H3, H5, H7 sequences (subject) by entering their assession numbers and running “BLAST”

  1. Answer the following questions:

    • What is the closest sequence to H1, and what evidence supports this?
    • What is the most distantly related sequence to H1?
    • Do the results support the grouping of HA into Group 1 and Group 2?
    • Which sequences cluster together in the distance tree, and what does this suggest about their relationship?
Tip

The BLAST results window contains multiple reports. Feel free to explore them.
You can switch between different alignment views; for example “Pairwise” and “Pairwise with dots for identity” to better visualize sequence similarities in NCBI BLAST. You can download or save the BLAST results!

2. Artemis

Artemis is a DNA viewer and annotation tool, free to download and use, written by Kim Rutherford from the Sanger Institute (Rutherford et al., 2000). The program allows the user to view a range of files, from simple sequence files (e.g. fasta format) to EMBL/Genbank entries, as well as the results of sequence analyses, in a highly interactive and intuitive graphical format. Artemis is routinely used for annotation and analysis of both prokaryotic and eukaryotic genomes, and can also be used to visualize mapped data from next generation sequencing. Several types/sets of information can be viewed simultaneously within different contexts. For example, Artemis gives you the two views of the same genome region, so you can zoom in to inspect detailed DNA sequence motifs, and also zoom out to view local gene architecture (e.g. operons), or even an entire chromosome or genome, all within one screen. It is also possible to perform analyses within Artemis and save the output for future reference.

The aim of this module is to become familiar with the basic functions of Artemis using a series of worked examples. These examples are designed to take you through the most immediately useful functions. However, there will be time, and encouragement, for you to explore other menus; features of Artemis that are not described in the exercises in this manual, but which may be of particular interest to some users.

Like all the Modules in this workshop, please remember: IF YOU DON’T UNDERSTAND, PLEASE ASK!

2.1.3 The basics of Artemis

Now you have an Artemis window open let’s look at what is in there.

  1. Drop-down menus: There’s lots in there so don’t worry about all the details right now.

  2. Entry (top line): shows which entries are currently loaded with the default entry highlighted in yellow (this is the entry into which newly created features are created). Selected feature: the details of a selected feature are shown here; in this case gene ulaG.

  3. Main sequence view panel: The central 2 grey lines represent the forward (top) and reverse (bottom) DNA strands. Above and below those are the 3 forward and 3 reverse reading frames. Stop codons are marked on the reading frames as black vertical bars. Genes and other annotated features (eg. Pfam and Prosite matches) are displayed as coloured boxes. We often refer to predicted genes as coding sequences or CDSs.

  4. Codon translation panel: This panel has a similar layout to the main panel but is zoomed in to show nucleotides and amino acids. Double click on a CDS in the main view to see the zoomed view of the start of that CDS. Note that both this and the main panel can be scrolled left and right (7, below) zoomed in and out (6, below).

  5. Feature panel: This panel contains details of the various features, listed in the order that they occur on the DNA. Any selected features are highlighted. The list can be scrolled (8, below).

  6. Sliders for zooming view panels.

  7. Sliders for scrolling along the DNA.

  8. Slider for scrolling feature list.

2.1.4 Getting around in Artemis

There are three main ways of getting to a particular DNA region in Artemis:

A. the Goto drop-down menu
B. the Navigator
C. the Feature Selector (2.3 Artemis Practice 3)

The best method depends on what you’re trying to do. Knowing which one to use comes with practice.

A. The ‘Goto’ menu

The functions on this menu (below the Navigator option) are shortcuts for getting to locations within a selected feature or for jumping to the start or end of the DNA sequence. This is really intuitive so give it a try!

It may seem that ‘Goto’ > ‘Start of Selection’, and ‘Goto’ > ‘Feature Start’ do the same thing.
Well they do if you have a feature selected but ‘Goto’ > ‘Start of Selection’ will also work for a region which you have selected by click-dragging in the main window. So yes, give it a try!

ImportantSuggested tasks
  1. Zoom out, select/highlight a large region of sequence by clicking the left hand button and dragging the cursor then go to the start and end of this selected region.
  2. Select a CDS then go to the start and end.
  3. Go to the start and end of the genome sequence.
  4. Select a CDS. Within it, go to a base (nucleotide) and/or amino acid of your choice.
  5. Highlight a region then, from the right click menu, select ‘Zoom to Selection’.

B. The Navigator

Before you start this function. What are Keys and Qualifiers?

Well,
In genome annotation, “key” and “qualifier” play different roles.

Key (Feature Key) Qualifier

  • Defines what the feature is

  • Is a standardized label describing the biological feature

  • Examples: gene, CDS, mRNA, tRNA, source

  • Provides additional details about that feature

  • Adds descriptive or functional information

  • Written as /name=value

Example:

    CDS 1..1700 <------Key

        /gene= "HA" <------Qualifier
        /product="hemagglutinin" <------Qualifier
        /organism="Influenza A virus" <------Qualifier
        /note="surface glycoprotein" <------Qualifier

Now you are ready…!

The Navigator panel is fairly intuitive so open it up.

TipSuggestions for where-to-go
  1. ‘Goto Base’: Think of a number between 1 and 5574202 and go to that base (notice how the cursors on the horizontal sliders move with you).
  2. ‘Goto Feature with Gene Name’: Your favourite gene name (try ‘blaKPC’). How many blaKPC copies do you find?
  3. Use ‘Goto Feature With This Qualifier value’ to search the contents of all qualifiers for a particular term. For example using the word ‘beta-lactamase’ will take you to the next feature with the word ‘beta-lactamase’ in any of its qualifiers. Note how repeated clicking of the ‘Goto’ button takes you to the following beta-lactamase in the order that they occur on the chromosome.
  4. tRNA genes. Type ‘tRNA’ in the ‘Goto Feature With This Key’.
  5. Try ATGCGCGC in the ‘Find Base Pattern’.
  6. Try DINVG the ‘Find Amino acid String’. Note that it searches all six reading frames regardless of whether the amino acids are encoded or not.

In Artemis, a window size of about 20,000 bp is used for GC content plots because smaller windows can make the graph look very uneven and hard to interpret due to natural small changes in DNA sequence. Using a larger window smooths out these short fluctuations and makes overall patterns easier to see. This is important because unusual DNA regions, such as those acquired from other organisms, are usually larger stretches of DNA and tend to show a consistent difference in GC content that becomes clearer at this scale. 2.3 Artemis Practice 3

Now we can move to gene annotation and selecting/extracting features using the Feature Selector.

This reference genome was annotated using Bakta, but genome annotations are not always complete or fully accurate. Automated tools rely on existing databases and prediction algorithms, which may miss genes, assign incorrect functions, or label proteins as “hypothetical” when no close match is available. In addition, scientific knowledge is constantly evolving, and new research may reveal updated or more precise functions for previously annotated genes. Therefore, genome annotation should be considered an ongoing process that can be improved by manual curation, comparison with new data, and updated literature.

2.3.2 Extract features

Features in the genome annotation also can be selected and extracted using the Feature Selector.

The region that we will extract both DNA sequence and annotation is from 1439982..1459581, containing the two Tn4401d copies that we just annotated above.

First, use the “Select” function –> “Base Range” –> enter 1439982..1459581 –> click OK.
The whole region should be highlighted.

Then, chose “Select” –> “Features within selection”.
This will highlighed all selected features within this region for extraction.

Next, chose “Edit” –> Subsequence (and Features)

A new window will appear:

Note that the two entries on the grey ‘Entry’ line are now denoted “no name”. They represent the same information in the same order as the original Artemis window but simply have no assigned ‘Entry’ names. As the sub-sequence is now viewed in a new Artemis session, this prevents the original files (CP009771.dna and CP009771.gbk) from being over-written.

Because the two entries (“no name” and “no name”) contain different types of information: first entry with the DNA sequence and the second entry with annotations, we have two options: - Save them as two separate files (one for sequence, one for annotation), or - Merge them into a single file by copying all annotation features into the DNA sequence file, so that it contains both sequence and annotation information.

This is how we do the merging. First, select all features by using “Copy All” in the Feature Selection window.

Next, go to “Edit” –> “Copy Selected Features To”, then select “No name” (first entry).

Now we are ready to save the “No name” entry into a file containing both the DNA sequence and its annotations.

Go to “File” –> “Save an entry as” –> “GenBank format”, then select “No name” (first entry) (now containing both DNA sequence and annotation) and enter the file name “blaKPC_transposon_Tn4401d”.

Now, open the newly saved file in Artemis to verify it!!!
